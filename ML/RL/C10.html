Chapter 10 Actor-Critic Methods

In chapter 8, we introduce value approximation function, that is to replace tabular representations for state/action value with function. Similarly, in chapter 9 we use function to represent policy instead fo tabular and turn to policy-based methods. So in this chapter we combine both of them, representing both value and policy with function and incorporating both policy-based and value-based methods.

Here, an “actor” refers to a policy update step. The reason that it is called an actor is that the actions are taken by following the policy. Here, an “critic” refers to a value update step. It is called a critic because it criticizes the actor by evaluating its corresponding values. From another point of view, actor-critic methods are still policy gradient algorithms. They can be obtained by extending the policy gradient algorithm introduced in Chapter 9.

Q actor-critic (QAC)

As actor-critic is still policy gradient method, it still need metrics to be optimized. Revisit the idea of policy gradient introduced in the last lecture. A scalar metric J(θ)J(\theta)J(θ), which can be vˉπ\bar{v}_\pivˉπ​ or rˉπ\bar{r}_\pirˉπ​. The gradient-ascent algorithm maximizing J(θ)J(\theta)J(θ) is

θt+1=θt+α∇θJ(θt)=θt+αES∼η,A∼π[∇θln⁡π(A∣S,θt)qπ(S,A)](1) \begin{aligned} \theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta_t) \\ &= \theta_t + \alpha \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta, A \sim \pi} \left[ \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|S, \theta_t) q_\pi(S, A) \right] \end{aligned} \tag{1} θt+1​​=θt​+α∇θ​J(θt​)=θt​+αES∼η,A∼π​[∇θ​lnπ(A∣S,θt​)qπ​(S,A)]​(1)

The stochastic gradient-ascent algorithm is

θt+1=θt+α∇θln⁡π(at∣st,θt)qt(st,at).(2) \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a_t|s_t, \theta_t) \color{blue}{q_t(s_t, a_t)}. \tag{2} θt+1​=θt​+α∇θ​lnπ(at​∣st​,θt​)qt​(st​,at​).(2)

  • If qt(st,at)q_t(s_t, a_t)qt​(st​,at​) is estimated by Monte Carlo learning, the corresponding algorithm is called REINFORCE or Monte Carlo policy gradient, which has already been introduced in Chapter 9 (but without value approximation function).

  • If qt(st,at)q_t(s_t, a_t)qt​(st​,at​) is estimated by TD learning, the corresponding algorithms are usually called actor-critic. Therefore, actor-critic methods can be obtained by incorporating TD-based value estimation into policy gradient methods.

The critic corresponds to the value update step via the Sarsa algorithm. The action values are represented by a parameterized function q(s,a,w)q(s,a,w)q(s,a,w). The actor corresponds to the policy update step in (2).

Advantage actor-critic (A2C)

The core idea of this algorithm is to introduce a baseline to reduce estimation variance.

How to do this? We need first learn about the property of baseline:

ES∼η,A∼π[∇θln⁡π(A∣S,θt)qπ(S,A)]=ES∼η,A∼π[∇θln⁡π(A∣S,θt)(qπ(S,A)−b(S))],(3) \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta, A \sim \pi} \left[ \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|S, \theta_t) q_\pi(S, A) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta, A \sim \pi} \left[ \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|S, \theta_t) (q_\pi(S, A) - b(S)) \right], \tag{3} ES∼η,A∼π​[∇θ​lnπ(A∣S,θt​)qπ​(S,A)]=ES∼η,A∼π​[∇θ​lnπ(A∣S,θt​)(qπ​(S,A)−b(S))],(3)

where the additional baseline b(S)b(S)b(S) is a scalar function of SSS. If the equation holds true, we only need to prove

ES∼η,A∼π[∇θln⁡π(A∣S,θt)b(S)]=0. \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta, A \sim \pi} \left[ \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|S, \theta_t) b(S) \right] = 0. ES∼η,A∼π​[∇θ​lnπ(A∣S,θt​)b(S)]=0.

This equation is valid because

ES∼η,A∼π[∇θln⁡π(A∣S,θt)b(S)]=∑s∈Sη(s)∑a∈Aπ(a∣s,θt)∇θln⁡π(a∣s,θt)b(s)=∑s∈Sη(s)∑a∈A∇θπ(a∣s,θt)b(s)=∑s∈Sη(s)b(s)∑a∈A∇θπ(a∣s,θt)=∑s∈Sη(s)b(s)∇θ∑a∈Aπ(a∣s,θt)=∑s∈Sη(s)b(s)∇θ1=0. \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta, A \sim \pi} \left[ \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|S, \theta_t) b(S) \right] &= \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \eta(s) \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a|s, \theta_t) \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a|s, \theta_t) b(s) \\ &= \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \eta(s) \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \nabla_\theta \pi(a|s, \theta_t) b(s) \\ &= \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \eta(s) b(s) \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \nabla_\theta \pi(a|s, \theta_t) \\ &= \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \eta(s) b(s) \nabla_\theta \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a|s, \theta_t) \\ &= \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \eta(s) b(s) \nabla_\theta 1 = 0. \end{aligned} ES∼η,A∼π​[∇θ​lnπ(A∣S,θt​)b(S)]​=s∈S∑​η(s)a∈A∑​π(a∣s,θt​)∇θ​lnπ(a∣s,θt​)b(s)=s∈S∑​η(s)a∈A∑​∇θ​π(a∣s,θt​)b(s)=s∈S∑​η(s)b(s)a∈A∑​∇θ​π(a∣s,θt​)=s∈S∑​η(s)b(s)∇θ​a∈A∑​π(a∣s,θt​)=s∈S∑​η(s)b(s)∇θ​1=0.​

Then such a expression has the same expectation with our previous metrics. Our next step is to choose a proper b(s)b(s)b(s) to reduce the approximation variance when we use samples to approximate the true gradient. Let:

X(S,A)≐∇θln⁡π(A∣S,θt)[qπ(S,A)−b(S)].(4) X(S, A) \doteq \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|S, \theta_t)[q_\pi(S, A) - b(S)]. \tag{4} X(S,A)≐∇θ​lnπ(A∣S,θt​)[qπ​(S,A)−b(S)].(4)

Then, the true gradient is E[X(S,A)]\mathbb{E}[X(S, A)]E[X(S,A)]. Since we need to use a stochastic sample xxx to approximate E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X], it would be favorable if the variance var(X)\text{var}(X)var(X) is small. For example, if var(X)\text{var}(X)var(X) is close to zero, then any sample xxx can accurately approximate E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X]. On the contrary, if var(X)\text{var}(X)var(X) is large, the value of a sample may be far from E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X].Although E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X] is invariant to the baseline, the variance var(X)\text{var}(X)var(X) is not. In fact, the optimal baseline that minimizes is:

Although the baseline in (5) is optimal, it is too complex to be useful in practice. If the weight ∥∇θln⁡π(A∣s,θt)∥2\|\nabla_\theta \ln \pi(A|s, \theta_t)\|^2∥∇θ​lnπ(A∣s,θt​)∥2 is removed from (5), we can obtain a suboptimal baseline that has a concise expression:

b†(s)=EA∼π[qπ(s,A)]=vπ(s),s∈S. b^\dagger(s) = \mathbb{E}_{A \sim \pi}[q_\pi(s, A)] = v_\pi(s), \quad s \in \mathcal{S}. b†(s)=EA∼π​[qπ​(s,A)]=vπ​(s),s∈S.

This suboptimal baseline is indeed the state value!

Here,

is called the advantage function, which reflects the advantage of one action over the others. More specifically, note that vπ(s)=∑a∈Aπ(a∣s)qπ(s,a)v_\pi(s) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \pi(a|s) q_\pi(s, a)vπ​(s)=∑a∈A​π(a∣s)qπ​(s,a) is the mean of the action values. If δπ(s,a)>0\delta_\pi(s, a) > 0δπ​(s,a)>0, it means that the corresponding action has a greater value than the mean value. The stochastic version of (7) is

θt+1=θt+α∇θln⁡π(at∣st,θt)[qt(st,at)−vt(st)]=θt+α∇θln⁡π(at∣st,θt)δt(st,at),(8) \begin{aligned} \theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a_t|s_t, \theta_t)[q_t(s_t, a_t) - v_t(s_t)] \\ &= \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a_t|s_t, \theta_t)\delta_t(s_t, a_t), \end{aligned} \tag{8} θt+1​​=θt​+α∇θ​lnπ(at​∣st​,θt​)[qt​(st​,at​)−vt​(st​)]=θt​+α∇θ​lnπ(at​∣st​,θt​)δt​(st​,at​),​(8)

where st,ats_t, a_tst​,at​ are samples of S,AS, AS,A at time ttt. Here, qt(st,at)q_t(s_t, a_t)qt​(st​,at​) and vt(st)v_t(s_t)vt​(st​) are approximations of qπ(θt)(st,at)q_{\pi(\theta_t)}(s_t, a_t)qπ(θt​)​(st​,at​) and vπ(θt)(st)v_{\pi(\theta_t)}(s_t)vπ(θt​)​(st​), respectively.

The algorithm in (8) updates the policy based on the relative value of qtq_tqt​ with respect to vtv_tvt​ rather than the absolute value of qtq_tqt​. This can be further interpreted by:

  • If qt(st,at)q_t(s_t, a_t)qt​(st​,at​) and vt(st)v_t(s_t)vt​(st​) are estimated by TD learning, the algorithm is usually called advantage actor-critic (A2C).

It should be noted that the advantage function in this implementation is approximated by the TD error:

qt(st,at)−vt(st)≈rt+1+γvt(st+1)−vt(st). q_t(s_t, a_t) - v_t(s_t) \approx r_{t+1} + \gamma v_t(s_{t+1}) - v_t(s_t). qt​(st​,at​)−vt​(st​)≈rt+1​+γvt​(st+1​)−vt​(st​).

This approximation is reasonable as:

qπ(st,at)=E[Rt+1+γvπ(St+1)∣St=st,At=at], q_\pi(s_t, a_t) = \mathbb{E} \left[ R_{t+1} + \gamma v_\pi(S_{t+1})| S_t = s_t, A_t = a_t \right], qπ​(st​,at​)=E[Rt+1​+γvπ​(St+1​)∣St​=st​,At​=at​],

Thus:

qπ(st,at)−vπ(st)=E[Rt+1+γvπ(St+1)−vπ(St)∣St=st,At=at], q_\pi(s_t, a_t) - v_\pi(s_t) = \mathbb{E} \left[ R_{t+1} + \gamma v_\pi(S_{t+1}) - v_\pi(S_t) | S_t = s_t, A_t = a_t \right], qπ​(st​,at​)−vπ​(st​)=E[Rt+1​+γvπ​(St+1​)−vπ​(St​)∣St​=st​,At​=at​],

This expression of using the TD error helps a lot. One merit is that we only need to use a single neural network to represent vπ(s)v_\pi(s)vπ​(s). Otherwise, if δt=qt(st,at)−vt(st)\delta_t = q_t(s_t, a_t) - v_t(s_t)δt​=qt​(st​,at​)−vt​(st​), we need to maintain two networks to represent vπ(s)v_\pi(s)vπ​(s) and qπ(s,a)q_\pi(s, a)qπ​(s,a), respectively. When we use the TD error, the algorithm may also be called TD actor-critic. In addition, it is notable that the policy π(θt)\pi(\theta_t)π(θt​) is stochastic and hence exploratory. Therefore, it can be directly used to generate experience samples without relying on techniques such as ϵ\epsilonϵ-greedy.

Off-policy actor-critic

Importance sampling

Consider a random variable X∈XX \in \mathcal{X}X∈X. Suppose that p0(X)p_0(X)p0​(X) is a probability distribution. Our goal is to estimate EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X]. Suppose that we have some i.i.d. samples {xi}i=1n\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n{xi​}i=1n​.

  • First, if the samples {xi}i=1n\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n{xi​}i=1n​ are generated by following p0p_0p0​, then the average value xˉ=1n∑i=1nxi\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_ixˉ=n1​∑i=1n​xi​ can be used to approximate EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] because xˉ\bar{x}xˉ is an unbiased estimate of EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] and the estimation variance converges to zero as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞ (see the law of large numbers in Box 5.1 for more information).

  • Second, consider a new scenario where the samples {xi}i=1n\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n{xi​}i=1n​ are not generated by p0p_0p0​. Instead, they are generated by another distribution p1p_1p1​. Can we still use these samples to approximate EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X]? The answer is yes. However, we can no longer use xˉ=1n∑i=1nxi\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_ixˉ=n1​∑i=1n​xi​ to approximate EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] since xˉ≈EX∼p1[X]\bar{x} \approx \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_1}[X]xˉ≈EX∼p1​​[X] rather than EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X].

In the second scenario, EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] can be approximated based on the importance sampling technique. In particular, EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] satisfies:

EX∼p0[X]=∑x∈Xp0(x)x=∑x∈Xp1(x)p0(x)p1(x)x⏟f(x)=EX∼p1[f(X)].(9) \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X] = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p_0(x)x = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p_1(x) \underbrace{\frac{p_0(x)}{p_1(x)} x}_{f(x)} = \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_1}[f(X)]. \tag{9} EX∼p0​​[X]=x∈X∑​p0​(x)x=x∈X∑​p1​(x)f(x)p1​(x)p0​(x)​x​​=EX∼p1​​[f(X)].(9)

Thus, estimating EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] becomes the problem of estimating EX∼p1[f(X)]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_1}[f(X)]EX∼p1​​[f(X)]. Let:

fˉ≐1n∑i=1nf(xi). \bar{f} \doteq \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i). fˉ​≐n1​i=1∑n​f(xi​).

Since fˉ\bar{f}fˉ​ can effectively approximate EX∼p1[f(X)]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_1}[f(X)]EX∼p1​​[f(X)], it then follows from (9) that

EX∼p0[X]=EX∼p1[f(X)]≈fˉ=1n∑i=1nf(xi)=1n∑i=1np0(xi)p1(xi)⏟importance weightxi.(10) \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X] = \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_1}[f(X)] \approx \bar{f} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \underbrace{\frac{p_0(x_i)}{p_1(x_i)}}_{\text{importance weight}} x_i. \tag{10} EX∼p0​​[X]=EX∼p1​​[f(X)]≈fˉ​=n1​i=1∑n​f(xi​)=n1​i=1∑n​importance weightp1​(xi​)p0​(xi​)​​​xi​.(10)

Equation (10) suggests that EX∼p0[X]\mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_0}[X]EX∼p0​​[X] can be approximated by a weighted average of xix_ixi​. Here, p0(xi)p1(xi)\frac{p_0(x_i)}{p_1(x_i)}p1​(xi​)p0​(xi​)​ is called the importance weight. Why is it called importance sampling? Because we want to find p0p_0p0​. If we sample a xix_ixi​, and its probability is high under p0p_0p0​ but low under p1p_1p1​, it means that it appears a lot under p0p_0p0​ but very little under the current p1p_1p1​ sampling. The reason why we do not directly use p0p_0p0​ is that it is too complex to use, such as a neural network. By contrast, (10) merely requires the values of p0(xi)p_0(x_i)p0​(xi​) for some samples instead of the complex expression and thus is much easier to implement in practice.

The off-policy policy gradient theorem

Like the previous on-policy case, we need to derive the policy gradient in the off-policy case.

Suppose β\betaβ is the behavior policy that generates experience samples. Our aim is to use these samples to update a target policy π\piπ that can maximize the metric:

J(θ)=∑s∈Sdβ(s)vπ(s)=ES∼dβ[vπ(S)], J(\theta) = \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} d_\beta(s) v_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_{S \sim d_\beta}[v_\pi(S)], J(θ)=s∈S∑​dβ​(s)vπ​(s)=ES∼dβ​​[vπ​(S)],

where the state distribution ρ\rhoρ is

ρ(s)≐∑s′∈Sdβ(s′)Prπ(s∣s′),s∈S, \rho(s) \doteq \sum_{s' \in \mathcal{S}} d_\beta(s') \text{Pr}_\pi(s|s'), \quad s \in \mathcal{S}, ρ(s)≐s′∈S∑​dβ​(s′)Prπ​(s∣s′),s∈S,

where Prπ(s∣s′)=∑k=0∞γk[Pπk]s′s=[(I−γPπ)−1]s′s\text{Pr}_\pi(s|s') = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k [P_\pi^k]_{s's} = [(I - \gamma P_\pi)^{-1}]_{s's}Prπ​(s∣s′)=∑k=0∞​γk[Pπk​]s′s​=[(I−γPπ​)−1]s′s​ is the discounted total probability of transitioning from s′s's′ to sss under policy π\piπ.

Compared with on-policy case, off-policy version adds the importane weight, as behavior policy is not same as the target policy. For proof, see at book.

Based on the theorem, similarly, we can apply the baseline with advantage function to get the algorithm:

θt+1=θt+αθπ(at∣st,θt)β(at∣st)∇θln⁡π(at∣st,θt)δt(st,at) \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha_\theta \frac{\pi(a_t|s_t, \theta_t)}{\beta(a_t|s_t)} \nabla_\theta \ln \pi(a_t|s_t, \theta_t) \delta_t(s_t, a_t) θt+1​=θt​+αθ​β(at​∣st​)π(at​∣st​,θt​)​∇θ​lnπ(at​∣st​,θt​)δt​(st​,at​)

and hence:

The only difference is that state adheres to another behaviral policy and add an importance weight.

Deterministic actor-critic (DPG)

Up to now, the policies used in the policy gradient methods are all stochastic since it is required that π(a∣s,θ)>0\pi(a|s, \theta) > 0π(a∣s,θ)>0 for every (s,a)(s,a)(s,a) (requirement of logloglog). We can add the softmax function at the last layer of the neural network to achieve this. However, when action is continuous, the output is a deterministic value.

The deterministic policy is specifically denoted as:

a=μ(s,θ)≐μ(s) a = \mu(s, \theta) \doteq \mu(s) a=μ(s,θ)≐μ(s)

μ\muμ is a mapping from S\mathcal{S}S to A\mathcal{A}A. μ\muμ can be represented by, for example, a neural network with the input as sss, the output as aaa, and the parameter as θ\thetaθ.We may write μ(s,θ)\mu(s, \theta)μ(s,θ) in short as μ(s)\mu(s)μ(s).

The policy gradient theorems introduced before are merely valid for stochastic policies. If the policy must be deterministic, we must derive a new policy gradient theorem.

Deterministic policy gradient theorem

The gradient of J(θ)J(\theta)J(θ) is:

∇θJ(θ)=∑s∈Sη(s)∇θμ(s)(∇aqμ(s,a))∣a=μ(s)=ES∼η[∇θμ(S)(∇aqμ(S,a))∣a=μ(S)],(11) \begin{aligned} \nabla_\theta J(\theta) &= \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \eta(s) \nabla_\theta \mu(s) (\nabla_a q_\mu(s, a))|_{a=\mu(s)} \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta} \left[ \nabla_\theta \mu(S) (\nabla_a q_\mu(S, a))|_{a=\mu(S)} \right], \end{aligned} \tag{11} ∇θ​J(θ)​=s∈S∑​η(s)∇θ​μ(s)(∇a​qμ​(s,a))∣a=μ(s)​=ES∼η​[∇θ​μ(S)(∇a​qμ​(S,a))∣a=μ(S)​],​(11)

where η\etaη is a distribution of the states. This theorem is a summary of the results of deterministic policy.

Unlike the stochastic case, the gradient in the deterministic case shown in (11) does not involve the action random variable AAA. As a result, when we use samples to approximate the true gradient, it is not required to sample actions. Therefore, the deterministic policy gradient method is off-policy.

Based on the gradient given in Theorem, we can apply the gradient-ascent algorithm to maximize J(θ)J(\theta)J(θ):

θt+1=θt+αθES∼η[∇θμ(S)(∇aqμ(S,a))∣a=μ(S)]. \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha_\theta \mathbb{E}_{S \sim \eta} \left[ \nabla_\theta \mu(S) (\nabla_a q_\mu(S, a))|_{a=\mu(S)} \right]. θt+1​=θt​+αθ​ES∼η​[∇θ​μ(S)(∇a​qμ​(S,a))∣a=μ(S)​].

The corresponding stochastic gradient-ascent algorithm is

θt+1=θt+αθ∇θμ(st)(∇aqμ(st,a))∣a=μ(st). \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha_\theta \nabla_\theta \mu(s_t) (\nabla_a q_\mu(s_t, a))|_{a=\mu(s_t)}. θt+1​=θt​+αθ​∇θ​μ(st​)(∇a​qμ​(st​,a))∣a=μ(st​)​.

It should be noted that this algorithm is off-policy since the behavior policy β\betaβ may be different from μ\muμ. First, the actor is off-policy. We already explained the reason when presenting Theorem. Second, the critic is also off-policy. Special attention must be paid to why the critic is off-policy but does not require the importance sampling technique. In particular, the experience sample required by the critic is (st,at,rt+1,st+1,a~t+1)(s_t, a_t, r_{t+1}, s_{t+1}, \tilde{a}_{t+1})(st​,at​,rt+1​,st+1​,a~t+1​), where a~t+1=μ(st+1)\tilde{a}_{t+1} = \mu(s_{t+1})a~t+1​=μ(st+1​). The generation of this experience sample involves two policies. The first is the policy for generating ata_tat​ at sts_tst​, and the second is the policy for generating a~t+1\tilde{a}_{t+1}a~t+1​ at st+1s_{t+1}st+1​. The first policy that generates ata_tat​ is the behavior policy since ata_tat​ is used to interact with the environment. The second policy must be μ\muμ because it is the policy that the critic aims to evaluate. Hence, μ\muμ is the target policy. It should be noted that a~t+1\tilde{a}_{t+1}a~t+1​ is not used to interact with the environment in the next time step. Hence, μ\muμ is not the behavior policy. Therefore, the critic is off-policy.How to select the function q(s,a,w)q(s, a, w)q(s,a,w)? The original research work [74] that proposed the deterministic policy gradient method adopted linear functions: q(s,a,w)=ϕT(s,a)wq(s, a, w) = \phi^T(s, a)wq(s,a,w)=ϕT(s,a)w where ϕ(s,a)\phi(s, a)ϕ(s,a) is the feature vector. It is currently popular to represent q(s,a,w)q(s, a, w)q(s,a,w) using neural networks, as suggested in the deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) method.
